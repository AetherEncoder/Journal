Reflection.

 The event titled Big Data and IoT: Paving the Way to Our Future was a Balik Talent Lecture held on August 20, 2024, at 8:30 AM in the ATA Hall. This lecture was organized for the College of Computer Studies students, with the guest speaker being Mr. Abraham Johannes T. Tourbier, BSIT 2020, now a distinguished Senior Big Data Engineer at SmartPlement Singapore. The topic of the lecture was Big Data and IoT, which stands for the Internet of Things.

 During the lecture, I learned that IoT refers to a network of interconnected devices that collect and share data. This data accumulates to such a vast scale that it is termed Big Data, which requires analysis to unlock its potential. Mr. Tourbier shared how his company leverages IoT and Big Data to implement practical solutions, such as controlling lights in large rooms to conserve energy and measuring plant moisture levels to optimize watering and fertilization. These real-world applications showcased how powerful Big Data and IoT can be in solving everyday challenges, making the session highly insightful.

 This lecture expanded my understanding of how information can be utilized not just as a source of knowledge but as a tool to solve complex problems. It emphasized the importance of developing tools capable of analyzing Big Data, as it exceeds human capacity for manual processing. For the first time, I fully understood why the field is called Information Technology—because information has become the most valuable resource of our era. This realization has reshaped my perspective, highlighting the immense potential hidden in data waiting to be harnessed.

 Because of this activity, I have developed a deeper appreciation for data as an invaluable resource with infinite potential. It has inspired me to explore its possibilities and to value the role of technology in transforming raw information into meaningful solutions.

Reflection.

 CodeQuest took place on August 22, 2024, at 1:00 PM in Room CC05. The event was a programming competition with participants forming teams of 1 to 3 members. I participated as part of a team named "Jay and His Servants." Our task was to solve three programming problems of varying difficulty—easy, medium, and hard—using the C language. The experience was intense and challenging, providing a chance to test our programming skills under time constraints.

 When I first read the "easy" problem, I felt optimistic about solving it. However, I stumbled on string comparison, mistakenly thinking the == operator would work. Normally, this would have been the end of our journey. Fortunately, the organizers noticed that all teams were struggling and allowed us to use the internet for reference (AI assistance was still prohibited). This rule change enabled us to make significant progress on the medium problem, although we skipped the easy and hard ones due to time limitations. When the competition ended, we assumed defeat, but surprisingly, we were declared the winners. Despite the victory, I didn’t feel any pride; our performance fell far short of excellence.

 This experience reshaped my understanding of the difference between being the best and being truly good. Winning didn’t feel like an achievement but rather an acknowledgment that none of the participants—including my team—were prepared enough. It was a humbling reminder of how vast the field of programming is and how much we still need to learn. This competition wasn’t a triumph but an eye-opener, pushing me to reflect on my current skill level and the effort required to improve. I now see this event as a stepping stone toward becoming a programmer I can truly be proud of.

 Because of this activity, I have adopted a new mindset: being the best is the bare minimum, while striving for constant improvement is the ultimate goal. This experience has ignited a fire within me to work harder, grow as a programmer, and return to future competitions with skills and confidence worthy of celebration.
